The Complex Exponential

A single function, \(e^{st}\), carries both oscillation and growth at once. That is why it solves almost every linear differential equation — and why every equation of motion is written in its language.

published 2026-06-23
version v0.1.0
visits

    Almost every linear system in physics — a mass on a spring, a swinging pendulum, an RLC circuit, a building swaying in the wind — obeys a differential equation that relates a quantity to its own rate of change. Solving those equations could be a thicket of trigonometry and calculus. Instead, almost all of it collapses onto a single function: \(e^{st}\), the complex exponential. Its secret is that it folds two seemingly unrelated behaviours — steady oscillation and runaway growth or decay — into one tidy expression governed by a single complex number \(s\).

    One Number, Two Behaviours

    Let the exponent \(s\) be a complex number, \(s = \sigma + i\omega\). Split the exponential along its real and imaginary parts and Euler's formula does the rest:

    \[e^{st} = e^{(\sigma + i\omega)t} = \underbrace{e^{\sigma t}}_{\text{envelope}}\,\bigl(\underbrace{\cos\omega t + i\sin\omega t}_{\text{rotation}}\bigr)\]

    Two knobs, two jobs. The real part \(\sigma\) drives the real exponential \(e^{\sigma t}\) — a pure envelope that swells when \(\sigma>0\), shrinks when \(\sigma<0\), and holds steady when \(\sigma=0\). The imaginary part \(\omega\) drives \(\cos\omega t + i\sin\omega t\) — a point spinning around the unit circle at angular rate \(\omega\). Multiply them and you get a vector whose length follows the envelope while its phase rotates: a spiral.

    The whole of \(s\) lives in one picture, the complex \(s\)-plane. Horizontal position is \(\sigma\) (growth or decay); vertical position is \(\omega\) (rate of oscillation). Slide \(s\) right and the response runs away; slide it left and the response dies out; sit it on the vertical axis and you get a pure, undying oscillation. Every distinct motion of a linear system is just a point — or a pair of points — somewhere on this plane.

    Watch It Circle the Time Axis

    The clearest way to feel this is to plot \(e^{st}\) in three dimensions. Run time \(t\) along the longitudinal axis, and let the real and imaginary parts of \(e^{st}\) spread out into the two transverse directions. The curve \(\bigl(t,\ e^{\sigma t}\cos\omega t,\ e^{\sigma t}\sin\omega t\bigr)\) corkscrews around the time axis — rotating because of \(\omega\), while its distance from the axis is the exponential envelope \(e^{\sigma t}\) (drawn dashed).

    Drag the point \(s\) around the plane on the left and watch the spiral on the right respond. Push it right to make the spiral flare open; pull it left to make it wind down to nothing; raise or lower it to wind the turns tighter or looser; drop it onto the real axis to kill the rotation entirely.

    Why Differential Equations Love It

    The reason \(e^{st}\) is everywhere is one line of calculus: differentiating it just multiplies it by \(s\).

    \[\frac{d}{dt}\,e^{st} = s\,e^{st}\]

    The function is its own derivative, up to a constant — it is an eigenfunction of the derivative. So when you feed \(e^{st}\) into any linear constant-coefficient differential equation, every derivative turns into a power of \(s\), and the calculus collapses into ordinary algebra. The equation of motion of a damped spring–mass system,

    \[m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = 0,\]

    becomes, on substituting \(x = e^{st}\), the simple polynomial \(\,ms^2 + cs + k = 0\). Its roots are exactly the values of \(s\) the system is allowed to move at — the points you have been dragging on the plane. A negative real part means the motion decays; an imaginary part means it rings; together they describe every free response the system can have.
